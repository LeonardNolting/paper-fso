Conceptually, computing collisions of a line with the obstruction model requires calculating which pixels of the DSM raster it crosses and checking if the surface at any of those pixels is higher than the line.

Line rasterization algorithms from computer graphics, such as Bresenham's and Xiaolin Wu's algorithm, solve a similar problem of painting pixels between two lines [TODO quote].

NVIDIA and other graphics processing unit producers offer dedicated ray tracing offloading cores and SDKs. For instance, NVIDIA RTX GPUs can be programmed with the OptiX SDK by building a BVH (bounding volume hierarchy) acceleration structure (AS) in memory and running line-of-sight checks against that almost entirely in hardware.
Our tests show that the main bottleneck becomes the memory transfer of the DSM to the accelerator, the time spent on building the AS and the additional memory occupied by the AS.
The DSM can be seen as a two-dimensional grid of ``pixel pillars'' - each pixel a cuboid stretching from -inf to the surface height at the pixel.
Converting the DSM to triangle primitives without quality loss requires two triangles per visible surface, which are laid out as one rectangle.
Since the top surface of a pixel is always visible, and the bottom pixel is always covered by the sides of this and neighboring pixels and is never visible, and assuming that on average two sides are visible per pixel, this results in six triangles per pixel.
... 3 floats per triangle, 1 after compaction, too many values, memory inflation ...
Using instance acceleration structures (IAS), which allow replicating one AS by shifting and scaling it, may reduce this memory inflation.
NVIDIA supports custom primitives for its ray tracing platform for any objects that cannot be modeled adequately with triangles.
Custom primitives assign an axis-aligned bounding box (AABB) containing the primitive and a custom shader, which will be invoked when a ray intersects the AABB. The ray tracing check is still offloaded and only hands over to the shader for an intersection with the AABB.
Since aforementioned ``pixel pillars'' are AABBs, they can be transformed to custom primitives.
Whenever the intersection shader is called, it already marks an intersection without further computation, and the line can immediately be marked as blocked.
Memory inflation can also be combated by smoothing out the model creating a triangulated RSG (regular square grid).

Given those parameters, 12.2\% of all candidate links are unobstructed and therefore theoretically suitable for an FSO link.
As worst-case free-space optical link quality degrades with increasing link length, our focus lies on short links.
On one side, the probability of an obstacle blocking the LoS of a link grows with increasing link length, favoring short links over long links.
On the other side, the further out you go from each individual node, the more candidate links can be found for it, leading to more long candidate links.
Both effects in combination lead to the distribution shown in~\cref{fig:link_lengths_pdf}, which particularly shows that only a small fraction of clear links are <400m long.

This is an inherent limitation of the relaying approach during worst-case conditions.
However, FSO's unique ability to reconfigure its links by physically steering the transmitter to point at another receiver can be used to create shortcuts in the path and reduce the hop count.
Reconfiguration happens in ``time scales of ms-s''~\cite{invited}.
This rewiring process is also known as the Small World phenomenon~\cite{smallworld}.
Key insight of the Watts and Strogatz model is that a highly clustered graph only needs a small percentage of shortcut links to achieve a high reduction in the ``average degrees of separation'', here hop count, while maintaining the clustering.~\cref{fig:hop_counts_rewired} shows that on our connected component by randomly selecting lines in the topology, removing them from the graph and adding a new, random line of up to $10km$ length to simulate a best-case scenario.
Availability of such long links is shown in~\cref{fig:link_lengths_pdf}.
Each step is run in 4 samples and the variation between those runs indicated with confidence markers.
We see that only rewiring 1\% of links halves the average hop count, rewiring 10\% reduces it to a third.

So far, we considered the worst-case and the best-case.
For average-case analysis, we simulate link reconfiguration by replacing links with new ones of more limited range.
This simulates conditions that don't allow for links of $10km$ or more, but also don't require minimal ranges only.
That translates, for instance, to average rain or light fog.
~\cref{fig:hop_counts_rewired} already includes several lines for average hop counts after rewiring with various maximum link lengths.
We see that reconfiguring links from $<300m$ to $<1000m$ has limited effects, but can at least halve the average hop count if every tenth link is reconfigured.
Furthermore, there is not much benefit to starting to use of links of more than $5km$.
~\cref{fig:hop_counts_1_percent} shows the full distributions of path lengths at a fix 1\% rewiring probability.
Even when reconfigured links are of unlimited range, there are still hop counts of 40 and higher.

Next to $300m$, we also consider the connected component made up of links of up to $1000m$ and use the same graphs.
We find a similar path length distribution, with a slightly lower average (20) and longer tail (running up to 80).
Reconfiguring even every tenth link to twice the length, $2km$, only decreases the average hop count to 14.
However, we see in~\cref{fig:hop_counts_1_percent_1000m} vastly improved results for 1\% reconfigurations with links of unlimited length and $10km$.
Very few to no paths are longer than 20 hops and the average drops below 10.
