    \subsection{Resilience}\label{subsec:resilience}
    For stronger connectivity guarantees, edge connectivity analysis shows how many link failures a connected component can tolerate the least while staying connected.
    Menger's theorem additionally states that in a k-edge connected graph, any pair of nodes is connected by k disjoint paths.
    Comparing to the previous connected component based on the combined network of two operators, a two-edge connected component that covers the city of Paris appears at 430 m.
    With nodes from all four operators, only links up to 360 m are needed.

    While k-edge connected components are protected from random link failures, FSO link failures resulting from LoS-interruptions are spatially correlated.
    Depending on the size of the obstacle and the angular diversity of links connecting nodes, a single obstacle can block several or all links of a node at once.

    \subsection{Hop Counts}\label{subsec:hop-counts}
    Nodes in a connected component can communicate with each other, but in the 300-m-topology, those paths often have 40 hops or more.
    This is an inherent limitation of the relaying approach during worst-case conditions.
    During best-case conditions, however, longer transmission ranges, such as 40 km, are shown to be possible~\cite{Willitsford:24}.
    FSO has the unique ability to reconfigure its link by physically steering the transmitter to point at another receiver in ``time scales of ms-s''~\cite{invited}.
    This rewiring process is also known as the Small World phenomenon~\cite{smallworld}.
    Key insight of the Watts and Strogatz model is that a highly clustered graph only needs a small percentage of shortcut links to achieve a high reduction in the ``average degrees of separation'', here hop count, while maintaining the clustering.
    Simulating a worst-case topology (e.g., heavy fog) transitioning to less bad conditions (e.g., light fog) by replacing 10\% of \textless300 m with links \textless1000 m halves the average path length.
    During clear, favorable conditions with 10\% link reconfigurations up to 5000 m, the average hop count shrinks to around 6, although extremes of 40 still exist.
    Running the same analysis on a connected component from \textless1000 m links during worst-case conditions, which is subsequently larger, rewiring 10\% to 2000 m barely reduces average hop counts by 1/4.
    Best-case reconfigurations up to 10000 m reduce the average to 5 hops, and barely any paths exceed 20 nodes.

    \subsection{Digital Surface Model}\label{subsec:digital-surface-model}
    LiDAR is generally well-suited for FSO LoS checks as they commonly share the same near-infrared band\todo{show / quote / is this necessary?}.
    The LiDAR impulse data by IGN has an average of $10\ impulses/m^2$ with vertical accuracy of $~10\ cm$ and horizontal accuracy of $~50\ cm$~\cite{lidar}.
    The DSM output is generally delivered at a grid space of $50\ cm$, lowering resolution by a factor of $2.5$.
    Modeling only the surface, it also crops information about the shape of objects underneath the surface, leading to any gaps through which a LoS could pass, for example through the Eiffel Tower or underneath bridges, not being modeled.

    The data has been collected since 2021 and constitutes only a momentary snapshot of obstructions.
    Volatile and future obstructions are not or inconsistently modeled, such as road vehicles, birds, new constructions and tree foliage of deciduous trees.

    A total of 0.046\% of the area considered takes on the ``nodata'' value, which we consider negligible.
    ``Zones Interdites à la Captation Aérienne des Données'' (ZICAD, restricted areas for aerial data collection) are either included in the nodata areas or censored through interpolation from surrounding data.
